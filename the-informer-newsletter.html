Cover Story · National Economy

Zimbabwe's Invisible Economy Steps Into the Light

A landmark national census reveals that three in every four businesses in Zimbabwe operate outside the formal system — and Bulawayo sits at the heart of the story.
By the Informer Editorial Desk · March 2026

For decades, it was the economy that official statistics struggled to count. The vendors lining Leopold Takawira Avenue, the welders in Makokoba, the seamstresses in Mzilikazi, the cross-border traders hauling goods through Beitbridge — all operating beyond the ledgers of ZIMRA, outside the gaze of regulators, yet sustaining millions of Zimbabwean households. Now, for the first time in the country's history, a comprehensive national economic census has put hard numbers to what many already knew: Zimbabwe is, in the most fundamental sense, an informal economy.

The Zimbabwe National Statistics Agency's landmark census — which surveyed over 204,798 businesses — concluded that 76% of all economic activity takes place outside formal structures. That figure is not merely a statistical footnote. It is a portrait of a nation, a testament to resilience, and an urgent call for policymakers to rethink how Zimbabwe approaches its own workforce.

Bulawayo, Zimbabwe's second city and industrial heartland, offers a striking contrast. The census shows that Bulawayo has the largest concentration of formal enterprises among all provinces — at 40%, far outpacing regions where the formal sector accounts for less than 30% of local market activity. Yet even here, in the corridors of what was once the region's manufacturing capital, the informal sector thrives, adapts, and grows.

"The informal economy is not a failure of ambition," said one market administrator in Bulawayo's Egodini terminus area, where thousands of traders, minibus operators, and food vendors converge daily. "It is what happens when the formal economy stops making space for ordinary people. We didn't choose this. We built this — because we had to."

The census paints a picture that is both vast and granular. Wholesale and retail trade dominates, accounting for 73% of all informal sector activity. Manufacturing accounts for a further 8%. Services — hairdressing, repairs, catering, transport — make up much of the rest. These are not marginal activities. They represent the daily economic engine of Zimbabwe's cities and towns.

Yet the Confederation of Zimbabwe Industries (CZI) struck a starkly different tone when the census results were released. Describing the situation as a "critical risk," CZI research officer MacDonald Mutengo argued that formal businesses are being squeezed — heavily regulated, taxed, and unable to compete with unregistered rivals who operate without the burden of compliance costs. "Companies are not making profits," he noted. "They are highly regulated." The warning is real, but critics argue that targeting the informal sector is misguided when the root causes — poor investment climate, currency instability, and regulatory complexity — remain unaddressed.

For researchers, the census data demands a more nuanced reading. A 2024 study by the International Centre for Tax and Development, which surveyed 2,490 informal operators across Harare and Masvingo, found that only one in five informal business operators earns enough to exceed the poverty line. The majority are not tax evaders thriving in the shadows — they are workers surviving at the margins, often paying more in levies, permits, and unofficial fees than the formal tax system would demand of them.

The story of Zimbabwe's informal economy is inseparable from the story of its recent decades: the hyperinflation of the 2000s that wiped out savings overnight; the collapse of formal employment that sent millions into self-employment by necessity; the successive currency crises that eroded trust in every financial institution. Informality is not a choice. It is, as researchers put it, "informal is normal" — the rational response of a workforce that has been let down by the formal structures that were supposed to serve it.

"For every five informal business operators, only one earns enough to rise above the poverty line. These are people trying to make ends meet — not freeloaders."

— International Centre for Tax and Development, 2024 Study

Analysis

In Depth

The Taxation Trap

Zimbabwe's government faces a fiscal paradox. The informal economy is enormous — contributing an estimated two-thirds of GDP — yet its contribution to national revenue remains minimal. The instinctive response has been to increase enforcement: raids on markets, licensing crackdowns, presumptive taxes applied broadly. But new research from the ICTD suggests this approach is self-defeating. Most informal traders are already heavily burdened by unofficial levies, council fees, and transport charges. Adding formal tax obligations without simplification simply drives businesses deeper underground — or out of business altogether. The solution, researchers argue, is not punishment but partnership: simplified flat-rate tax regimes, phased licensing, and a genuine reduction in the cost of doing business formally.

The Gender Dimension

Women account for 52% of Zimbabwe's informal workforce — but they occupy the sector's most precarious positions. World Bank analysis of the 2017 PICES survey found that informal communal farmers, a group with a high proportion of women, experience extreme poverty at rates of 42%, compared with 15% for other informal workers. Women in the informal economy face compounding disadvantages: limited access to microfinance, no collateral for credit, insecure trading spaces, and near-total exclusion from social protection schemes. Only 12% of informal workers live in households where any member benefits from a government safety net. For the Bulawayo Public Library, understanding these dynamics is central to any meaningful community development agenda. Women's economic empowerment and informal sector reform are not separate conversations — they are one.

Bulawayo's Double Role

Bulawayo's prominence in the formal economy — 40% of all formal enterprises — might suggest insulation from informalisation pressures. The reality is more complex. The city's manufacturing heritage has shrunk dramatically since the 1990s, as factory closures pushed thousands of skilled workers into the informal economy. Today, Bulawayo's informal sector is characterised by higher-skilled activity than other provinces: light engineering, garment production, food processing, and artisanal trades that bear the marks of a once-industrialised workforce. This creates both challenges and opportunities. The skills base is there. The market connections exist. What is missing is the enabling environment — access to factory space, affordable credit, and regulatory clarity — that could help Bulawayo's informal producers make the transition into viable, registered enterprises.

Profile & Interview

The Market Mover

In Conversation

"I don't call myself informal. I call myself a businesswoman. The government calls me informal because it hasn't figured out how to deal with me yet."

The Informer: You've been operating in Bulawayo for over twenty years. How has the environment changed?
It has changed in every way except the one that matters most. When I started, I had one sewing machine and I was sewing from a room in my mother's house in Mzilikazi. The city council came once, threatened to arrest me for running a business without a licence, and then never came back. That was my relationship with authority — invisibility with occasional harassment. Now I have seven employees and a proper workshop space. But the relationship with authority is still the same. We pay council levies for our market space. We pay for the electricity connection. We pay informal taxes to people who come with clipboards and collect money whose destination I have never understood. What we do not get is any recognition, any banking relationship, any path to growing into something the government would call formal. The goalposts are always moving.
The Informer: Your business supplies school uniforms to three private schools. How do you manage without being formally registered?
Relationships. In Zimbabwe, relationships are your business licence. The school administrators know me. They have been buying from me for years. They know the quality of my work, they know I deliver on time, they know that if there is a problem I will fix it without argument. When they need a receipt, I write them one. When they need a quote on letterhead, my daughter designed one on the computer. We are practical people. We solve problems with what we have. The formal system was not designed for people like me, so I designed around it. But I will tell you honestly — it costs me. I cannot get a bank loan on my order history. I cannot bid for government contracts. I cannot get health insurance for my workers. These are real losses.
The Informer: What would it take for you to formalise your business?
Make it cheap. Make it simple. Make it worth something. Right now, to register a business in Zimbabwe, you need multiple documents, multiple offices, multiple fees, and multiple weeks. And at the end of it, what do I get? I get the right to pay ZIMRA. That is the offer. Nobody is saying: here is a business development grant, here is a simplified bank account, here is access to a market where government contracts are open to you. Until formalisation comes with benefits, not just obligations, most of us will stay where we are. And I don't blame us for it.
Grace Dube · Garment manufacturer & informal sector trader, Bulawayo · Operating since 2004
Name used with permission. Interview conducted in Ndebele and translated.

Column · Technology & Finance

EcoCash Changed Everything — And Nobody Planned for It

There is a quiet revolution that has been underway in Zimbabwe's informal economy for over a decade, and it has nothing to do with government policy. It is mobile money. When EcoCash launched in 2011, it was aimed at the unbanked — at exactly the traders, vendors, and small producers who make up Zimbabwe's informal majority. What happened next was not planned by any ministry or development agency. Informal traders adopted mobile money faster than any other segment of the economy. They used it to pay suppliers, receive payments from customers, save small amounts safely, and send remittances to family in rural areas. By the time the formal banking sector understood what had happened, the informal economy already had its own financial infrastructure — accessible, low-cost, and trusted in a way that banks had never managed to be. The irony is profound. The state spent years trying to bring informal traders into the financial system through licensing and taxation. A private mobile network company did it in three years, simply by making it easy. The lesson for policymakers is not subtle: meet people where they are, not where you wish they were.

Columnist: Sipho Ndlovu · Economist and former Bulawayo City Council adviser
Column · Policy & Governance

The Permit Trap: How Bureaucracy Criminalises Survival

To operate a food stall in a Bulawayo designated vending area, a trader technically needs a council permit, a health clearance certificate, a Zimbabwe Revenue Authority registration number, and in some cases proof of a fixed address for correspondence. The cost of obtaining all of these, in time and money, can exceed a month's earnings for a small-scale vendor. The result is predictable: most traders operate without them, not out of defiance but out of mathematical necessity. They then become vulnerable to intermittent enforcement — raids that confiscate stock, fines that must be paid immediately to avoid detention, and the constant low-level anxiety of operating under threat. This is not a tax system. It is a system of organised informality, where the state extracts resources from informal traders through enforcement rather than through fair, predictable, and proportionate taxation. The 2025 census data should prompt an urgent national conversation about decriminalising economic survival. Simplifying permits to a single, affordable annual fee — with phased payment options — would cost the government almost nothing and would transform the lives of hundreds of thousands of Zimbabweans.

Columnist: Ruramai Chikwanda · Public policy researcher, Bulawayo
Column · Economic History

We Were Always Here: The Informal Economy Before the Crisis

There is a common misconception that Zimbabwe's informal economy is a product of the 2000s crisis — that hyperinflation, land reform, and economic mismanagement created the informal sector from nothing. This is historically wrong, and the error matters. Informal trading, cross-border commerce, and unregistered small enterprise have been part of Zimbabwean economic life since before independence. The colonial economy was itself built on a dual structure: a formal economy reserved for white settlers, and an informal survival economy in which the majority black population was compelled to operate. Post-independence policy largely inherited this structure without dismantling it. What the 2000s crisis did was dramatically expand the informal sector, as formal employment collapsed. But the informal economy's roots — its social networks, its mutual aid associations, its cultural logic of Ubuntu-based exchange — are far older and deeper than any particular political moment. Understanding this history matters because it tells us that the informal economy will not be reformed by treating it as a problem to be solved. It must be engaged as an economy in its own right, with its own institutions, its own strengths, and its own legitimate claims on public resources.

Columnist: Prof. Lindiwe Mpofu · Economic history, National University of Science & Technology, Bulawayo
News & Briefings

Around the Sector

Taxation

ZIMRA Eyes Presumptive Tax Expansion — Traders Wary

The Zimbabwe Revenue Authority is reportedly considering expanding its presumptive tax regime to cover additional categories of informal businesses. Traders in Bulawayo's Mhlahlandlela market expressed concern that flat-rate taxes imposed without income verification would disproportionately burden those already earning below the poverty line. Research suggests only 6% of informal operators currently pay presumptive tax, despite broader enforcement efforts.

Currency

ZiG Currency Adoption Slow in Informal Markets

Despite the government's push for the Zimbabwe Gold (ZiG) currency, informal traders across Bulawayo report continued preference for US dollar transactions. Cross-border traders cite predictability and international acceptability as key factors. The multi-currency system, extended until 2030, means informal traders must navigate multiple currencies simultaneously — adding complexity and cost to daily operations.

Infrastructure

Egodini Terminus Redevelopment: Traders Demand Consultation

Bulawayo's long-delayed Egodini Mall redevelopment continues to generate controversy. Informal traders who have operated in the area for decades say they have received no meaningful consultation on where they will trade once construction is completed. Traders' associations have called on the Bulawayo City Council to guarantee designated vending spaces in the new development and to provide advance notice before any relocations take place.

Gender

Women's Credit Access Remains Critical Barrier

A new report from the Women's University in Africa highlights that informal sector women in Zimbabwe face systematic exclusion from formal credit markets. Without title deeds, formal employment records, or business registration, women traders cannot access conventional bank loans. The report recommends community-based savings groups (mukando) be formally recognised as creditworthiness evidence by microfinance institutions.

Regional

Cross-Border Trade: Beitbridge Congestion Costs Traders Millions

Informal cross-border traders report that chronic congestion at Beitbridge border post is costing them significant income. Traders who bring goods from South Africa face waits of up to 48 hours, with perishable goods spoiling before they can clear customs. The Beitbridge Traders Association has called on ZIMRA to establish a dedicated lane for registered small-scale cross-border traders with simplified documentation requirements.

Technology

Digital Payments Transform Bulawayo's Informal Sector

Mobile money usage among Bulawayo's informal traders has reached levels that researchers describe as transformative. A local survey found that over 70% of traders in designated vending areas now accept EcoCash and other mobile payment platforms, up from under 30% five years ago. Digital transactions offer traders safety (less cash handling), traceability, and — crucially — a transaction history that could eventually support credit applications.

The Cross-Border Economy: How Zimbabweans Built a Trade Network That Governments Still Don't Understand

From the corridors of Beitbridge to the markets of Johannesburg, Lusaka, and Gaborone, Zimbabwean cross-border traders have constructed one of southern Africa's most sophisticated informal supply chains. They move clothing, electronics, household goods, and food across borders, navigating customs regulations, currency fluctuations, and logistical challenges that would defeat most registered import-export businesses. They do it without lawyers, without freight forwarders, and often without formal import licences — and they do it profitably enough to sustain families and communities. This is their story.
